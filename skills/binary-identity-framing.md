# Binary Identity Framing

Force the reader to choose which type of person they are. No middle ground.

## Why It Works

Humans are wired to self-categorize. When you present two clear identities, the reader instinctively picks one — and then acts to confirm that choice. It bypasses rational resistance by making the decision about *who they are*, not *what they should buy*.

## The Framework

1. **Name two types of people** (or businesses, marketers, etc.)
2. **Type A** = the desirable identity (what your reader wants to be)
3. **Type B** = the undesirable identity (what they fear becoming)
4. **Show Type A using your product/approach**
5. **Show Type B suffering without it**
6. **CTA:** "Choose which one you are"

## Template

```
Right now, there are two types of [audience]:

**Type A: [Name]** — [Desirable trait]. [How they operate]. [Result they get].

**Type B: [Name]** — [Undesirable trait]. [How they struggle]. [Result they suffer].

Today, you choose which one you become.
```

## Examples

**Blog intro (SEO/Marketing):**
"There are two types of SEO agencies in 2026: the ones building content systems with AI, and the ones still manually writing 800-word blog posts at $200 each. The first group is scaling. The second group is burning out."

**LinkedIn hook:**
"Two types of CMOs right now: those who've built a content engine, and those who are about to get replaced by one. Which one are you?"

**Landing page CTA:**
"Every business owner is either building systems or putting out fires. Click here to find out which camp you're in."

## Anti-Patterns

- ❌ Don't make Type B so extreme it feels unrealistic
- ❌ Don't use this more than once per piece — it loses power with repetition
- ❌ Don't forget to give Type B a path to Type A (your product is the bridge)
- ❌ Never insult Type B — they might be your current customer

## Combine With

- **Parallel Narrative** — Name your two types, then tell both stories
- **Future Pacing** — Show what Type A's life looks like after transformation
- **Exclusivity Positioning** — "Only 2% are Type A"
